A brief history of the city

Karakol (Przhevalsk) is a young city.
History has preserved for us the exact date, motives and circumstances of its occurrence.
This is one of the first settlements founded in the Issyk-Kul basin after Kyrgyzstan voluntarily joined Russia.
In 1865, the tsarist government hastily built a number of fortifications on the Kashgar border. One of them - Ak-Su - later became the center of the Karakul district. Ak-Su was located away from trade routes and seasonal migrations of livestock breeders, so it was decided to move the county center to a more convenient location. For this purpose, Colonel of the General Staff A.V. Kaulbars was sent to the basin.
After a thorough reconnaissance and questioning of local residents, it was decided to lay the new city on the old trade caravan road from the Chui Valley to Kashgaria, not far from the ruins of the old Kovand fortress in the lower reaches of the Karakol River.
"On July 1, 1869, the streets, squares and Gostiny dvor were laid, the lower crowns of the defensive barracks were also laid," A.V. Kaulbars wrote later in his "Remarks on the organization of Karakol". This date was considered the birthday of the city, which was originally named after the river - Karakol.
On October 20, 1888, on the way to his fifth trip to Central Asia, N. M. Przhevalsky died in the city. On November 1, Karakol saw him off on his last journey. In the early morning, the coffin with the body of the deceased, mounted on the carriage of a field gun, escorted by the fifth West Siberian linear battalion and a battery of mountain artillery, moved to Lake Issyk-Kul. Almost all the residents of the city followed the coffin in mournful silence.
The day before his death, Nikolai Mikhailovich, regaining consciousness, said: "I ask one thing: do not forget, bury me by all means on the shore of Issyk-Kul... Make the inscription simple: "Travelers. M. Przhevalsky", put it in the coffin in my expedition clothes."
The deceased's wish was fulfilled. The place for the grave was chosen on the dry high shore of the Karakul Bay, from where a charming view of the city, the lake and the surrounding mountain giants opens. A simple wooden cross with the inscription indicated by the deceased was placed on the grave.
On March 11, 1889, by decree of the tsar, Karakol was renamed the city of Przhevalsk. And on June 26, 1894, five years later, the grand opening of the monument erected at the grave of the deceased took place.
